## How to use these

- Each script has a **runtime estimate** (≈150 spoken words/minute), an
  **audience**, a **tone** note, and optional **on-screen** suggestions.
- **Paste only the text under "SCRIPT"** into HeyGen — that is the spoken
  narration. Everything in brackets or under "On-screen" is for you/editing, not
  to be read aloud.
- Scripts are written first-person ("I"/"we") for an avatar of you.
- Mix and match: Script 0 is a hook; 1–3 explain the concept; 4–5 are
  audience-specific pitches.

---

## Script 0 — Hook / teaser (~25–30s)

**Audience:** any · **Tone:** punchy, curious · **On-screen:** title card
"A newsroom in your pocket."

> **SCRIPT**
>
> What if you had an entire newsroom — in your pocket? Reporters that never
> sleep, scanning the world for the stories that actually matter to you. An
> editor that cuts the noise. And you — the human — still holding the pen.
> That's what we're building. Let me show you.

---

## Script 1 — The big idea (~70–80s)

**Audience:** any · **Tone:** warm, confident · **On-screen:** simple visuals —
a flood of headlines narrowing to a short, clean briefing.

> **SCRIPT**
>
> I want to tell you about something we've been building. We call it a newsroom
> in your pocket.
>
> Every day the world produces more news than any of us can read. Most of it is
> noise. And the few things that genuinely matter to you — a policy shift, a
> competitor's move, a story about your own community — get buried.
>
> So we built a team of digital reporters. Each one watches a specific beat.
> They search, they gather, and they bring back only what's significant — with
> the source attached, every time. Then a single editor cuts the duplicates and
> the fluff, and hands you a short, ranked briefing. Three times a day.
>
> And here's the most important part: it never publishes on its own. A human
> always writes the final word. The machine does the legwork. You bring the
> meaning.

## Script 2 — How it works (~80–90s)

**Audience:** any (slightly more technical) · **Tone:** clear, explanatory ·
**On-screen:** an old newsroom → modern dashboard; "reporters" then "editor".

> **SCRIPT**
>
> Here's how it actually works — and why it's different.
>
> Picture an old newspaper. You had reporters out on their beats, and an editor
> at the desk pulling it all together. We rebuilt that — with AI.
>
> Each "reporter" is its own focused agent. One watches AI policy. One watches
> our competitors. You can add a new beat in minutes — hire a new desk — and the
> others don't slow down.
>
> When they file their stories, a single editor pulls them together. And this
> part uses no expensive AI at all — it just strips out anything old, anything
> duplicated, anything that doesn't clear the bar. What's left is ranked:
> official announcements and the big stories first.
>
> It lands in a shared folder, with a live dashboard showing every beat and
> every story. And then it waits — for a human. Because the point was never to
> replace the writer. It was to free them.

## Script 3 — "Human AND" (the values) (~70–80s)

**Audience:** any · **Tone:** sincere, principled · **On-screen:** the words
"Human AND…" with the second word changing — machine, art, community.

> **SCRIPT**
>
> There's a phrase at the heart of this project. Human — and. We leave the second
> word open on purpose. Human and machine. Human and art. Human and community.
> Whatever comes after, the human is always in it.
>
> Here's what that means in practice. The agents do the mundane work — the
> searching, the sorting, the monitoring. The human does the work that matters —
> the judgement, the story, the message. Nothing gets published without a person
> reviewing it.
>
> When we use someone's photo, their art, their words — they get paid, fairly,
> with their name on it. Advertising never bends the story. We're not automating
> the creative out of the picture. We're handing them their time back.
>
> That's the whole idea. Elevate the human. Remove the mundane.

## Script 4 — Team pitch (internal) (~80–90s)

**Audience:** your team / colleagues · **Tone:** practical, energising ·
**On-screen:** calendar with two daily briefings; the dashboard.

> **SCRIPT**
>
> Quick pitch — for us, as a team.
>
> We spend hours every week just trying to stay current. Scanning the news.
> Tracking what our competitors are doing. Watching the policy landscape shift
> under our feet.
>
> What if that just… arrived? Three times a day, a short briefing — only the
> significant stuff, every item sourced, ranked by what matters. A live
> dashboard so anyone can see what's moving across every topic we care about.
>
> It runs on tools we already have, on a small budget, by design. And here's the
> part I care about most: it doesn't replace any of us. It takes the busywork off
> our plates so we can spend our time on judgement, on clients, on the actual
> thinking.
>
> Less scanning. More creating. I'd like us to pilot it on two topics, and see
> what a week feels like.

## Script 5 — Community pitch (~80–90s)

**Audience:** local community / civic groups · **Tone:** grounded, hopeful ·
**On-screen:** local imagery; a local storyteller at a laptop.

> **SCRIPT**
>
> I want to talk to you — our community — about something I think could matter
> here.
>
> Local stories get lost. The big outlets don't cover them, and the people who
> could tell them don't have the time or the tools.
>
> So imagine a small, tireless newsroom that watches for what's happening right
> here — and hands it to a real person to tell. It can bring in local
> photographers, and pay them fairly for their work. It can translate, so more of
> our neighbours can read it. It can make everything accessible by default.
>
> But it never speaks for us. A human from here always writes the final word.
>
> This isn't AI replacing local voices. It's AI giving local voices their time
> back — and a bigger reach. I'd love to show you what that could look like for
> us.
